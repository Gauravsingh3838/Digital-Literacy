Case Study: The $100 Million "Fake Invoice" ScamTarget: Finance departments at Google and Facebook (Meta).Timeline: 2013–2015Primary Technique: Business Email Compromise (BEC) and Spear Phishing.1. The Strategy: Impersonation & TrustThe attacker, Evaldas Rimasauskas, identified a legitimate business relationship between the tech giants and Quanta Computer, a Taiwan-based hardware manufacturer.The Bait: The attacker registered a company in Latvia with the same name ("Quanta Computer") and opened several bank accounts across Europe.The Hook: Using Spear Phishing, he sent meticulously crafted emails to finance employees at Google and Facebook. These emails mimicked the tone, formatting, and legal language of actual Quanta invoices.2. The Execution: Social EngineeringThe emails weren't just "spam"; they were highly targeted.Urgency: The invoices were presented as "overdue" or "urgent payment requests" for real hardware services provided to the companies.Spoofing: The attacker used email addresses that looked almost identical to legitimate corporate accounts, tricking employees into believing they were communicating with their established vendor.Documentation: To bypass internal audits, the attacker included forged contracts, letters, and even corporate seals that appeared to be signed by executives at the tech firms.3. The ImpactOver two years, the employees unknowingly wired a combined $100 million ($23M from Google, $98M from Facebook) into the attacker's fraudulent bank accounts.4. Lessons Learned & PreventionThis case is a classic example of how even the most technically advanced companies can be vulnerable to simple human-centric attacks.Weakness ExploitedModern Prevention (2026 Standards)Lack of VerificationOut-of-Band Verification: Always confirm large wire transfers via a secondary, known phone number—never via the email link provided.Single-Point FailureMulti-Person Approval: Require at least two different employees to sign off on any change to vendor bank details.Email TrustDMARC & AI Filters: Use advanced AI-driven email security that flags "look-alike" domains and anomalous sender behavior.Human ErrorSimulated Phishing Training: Regularly test employees with fake phishing campaigns to build "muscle memory" for spotting red flags.Red Flags to Watch For in 2026AI-Generated Content: Emails with perfect grammar but an unusual "robotic" flow.Deepfake Audio: A "boss" calling to confirm a transfer (always verify via a separate chat app).QR Code Phishing (Quishing): Scams hidden in QR codes that bypass traditional text-based email filters.
